Naxdom 250 is a BMX‑style bike that sits around the mid‑price point. It’s got a lightweight aluminum frame, a 16‑frame or 20‑frame frame depending on variant, and a 20‑inch wheel setup that’s pretty forgiving for tricks but still fast enough for street racing. The geometry is typical for a “street‑ready” BMX: short top tube, a fat seat tube for stability, and a short wheelbase that makes it easy to jack‑hop in tight corners.

Key bits people usually latch onto:

| Feature | What you see |
|---------|--------------|
| **Frame** | 6061‑al aluminum, 5‑mm thick, double‑cannulated for a tight feel. |
| **Wheels** | 20″ rear, 16″ front (or 20/20 combo on the “All‑Road” variant) with 2‑tire setup for traction. |
| **Drivetrain** | 1‑speed unless you opt for an off‑the‑shelf 20‑tooth cassette. |
| **Brakes** | V‑brakes on the front, mechanical disc on the rear (some models) – good stopping power for the sheer weight of BMX stunts. |
| **Pedals** | Hook‑style with 3‑section cleats for that ankle‑secured grip. |

People like it because:

* It’s substantially cheaper than the flagship Naxdom “250” line-up but still feels “built‑to‑last.”  
* The geometry makes it feel squat and leg‑strong, which is great when you want to do short, high‑angle jumps.  
* It’s roughly the same weight as a home‑built BMX—usually under 18 lb for the whole bike set‑up—so it’s still pick‑up in the street.

If you’re looking to buy, the main questions to ask are:

1. **Wheel size:** 16″ front wins for tricks, 20” gives you a smoother ride at higher speed.  
2. **Brakes:** Do you want a disc for a little more power or are you fine with V‑brakes?  
3. **Race‑grade or street‑grade:** The “race” variation will have a lighter seat tube; the “street” one is a bit more robust all‑around.

There’s also a “Naxdom 250 Moto” version that adds a derailleur and a multi‑speed chain, which some riders convert for touring. If you’re mostly doing tricks, the original single‑speed is adequate.
